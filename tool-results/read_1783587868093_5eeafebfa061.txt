    80→ Part 1: The Big Picture
    81→ The Periodic Table of AI Elements organises 48 essential AI concepts into 8 logical groups, mirroring how
    82→ chemical elements build the physical world. Just as hydrogen and oxygen combine to form water, AI elements like
    83→ Tokens, Prompts, and Models combine to form business systems. Understanding these elements individually is the
    84→ first step; understanding how they combine is where real business value emerges.
    85→
    86→ The table is not just a taxonomy. It is a map of leverage. Groups 1 through 4 represent the foundations: what AI is
    87→ and how it works. Groups 5 through 7 represent the connectors and builders: how you make AI work for you.
    88→ Group 8 ties everything back to business outcomes. The highest entrepreneurial value concentrates in Groups 5, 6,
    89→ and 7, what we call the High Leverage Zone, because this is where AI transforms from a tool you visit into a
    90→ system that works for you around the clock.

   146→                       Token How AI reads text, not words. ~1 token per 0.75 words. Directly affects cost, speed, and how much
   147→         Tk            you can feed an AI at once.
   148→        Token
   149→                          Real-World Example: A 1,000-word email is ~1,333 tokens. At $0.03/1K tokens, that costs ~$0.04.
   150→                         Combines with: Context Window, Model, Prompt
   151→                         Common mistakes: Confusing tokens with words; Ignoring token costs in automations; Not truncating inputs to fit
   152→                         context windows
   153→                         Reflection: Can you estimate the token cost of sending a 5-page document to an AI?
   154→
   155→
   156→                       Model The core AI engine. Different models have different strengths, costs, and capabilities. Choosing the
   157→       Md              right one is fundamental.
   158→        Model
   159→                          Real-World Example: Models are like vehicle engines: a city car for short trips, a truck for heavy loads.
   160→                          Don't use a truck to pop to the shops.
   161→                         Combines with: Parameters, Temperature, Fine-Tuning
   162→                         Common mistakes: Using the most expensive model for every task; Assuming all models handle every task equally;
   163→                         Not testing smaller models first
   164→                         Reflection: Can you name three AI models and when you would choose each?
   165→
   166→
   167→                       Prompt Your instructions to the model. Structure determines ~80% of output quality. The most underrated
   168→         Pr            skill for non-technical AI builders.
   169→        Prompt
   170→                          Real-World Example: 'Write a marketing email' gives anything. 'Write a 150-word email to SaaS founders
   171→                          promoting a 20% AI discount, friendly-professional tone, clear CTA by Friday' gives precision.
   172→                         Combines with: System Prompt, Temperature, RAG
   173→                         Common mistakes: Being vague and expecting precise results; Not iterating on prompts when output is poor; Blaming
   174→                         the model when the prompt is the issue
   175→                         Reflection: Can you rewrite a vague prompt into a specific one and see the difference?
   176→
   177→
   178→                       Context Window How much the model can see in a single conversation. Like short-term memory with a
   179→        Cw             hard limit.
   180→    Context Window
   181→                          Real-World Example: Imagine reading a book but only remembering the last 10 pages. GPT-4's 128K
   182→                          window holds ~96,000 words before things fade.
   183→                         Combines with: Token, Memory, RAG
   184→                         Common mistakes: Stuffing too much into one conversation; Not understanding why AI 'forgets'; Assuming bigger
   185→                         windows solve all problems
   186→                         Reflection: If your AI keeps forgetting instructions mid-conversation, which element do you investigate?
   187→
   188→
   189→                       Temperature Controls creativity vs. predictability. Low = precise, consistent. High = creative,
   190→        Tp             unpredictable.
   191→     Temperature
   192→                          Real-World Example: Legal contract clauses? Temperature near 0. Brainstorming marketing slogans? Crank
   193→                          to 0.8+ for wild ideas.
   194→                         Combines with: Prompt, Model, Guardrails
   195→                         Common mistakes: Using high temperature for automated processes; Expecting identical outputs above temperature 0;
   196→                         Not adjusting temperature when switching task types
   197→                         Reflection: What temperature for an AI drafting customer emails vs. one writing poetry?

   253→                       Embedding How AI converts text into numbers that capture meaning. Enables semantic understanding.
   254→        Em                Real-World Example: Like a library shelved by meaning, not alphabet. Search 'grow sales' and it finds
   255→      Embedding
   256→                          'revenue expansion' too.
   257→                         Combines with: Vector Database, RAG, Training Data
   258→                         Common mistakes: Confusing embeddings with keyword matching; Not understanding why semantic search beats
   259→                         text search; Ignoring embedding quality in search systems
   260→                         Reflection: Why might an embedding search find a document with none of your search words?

   262→
   263→                       Vector Database Where embeddings are stored and searched. If you build any AI that searches information,
   264→        Vd             you need a vector database.
   265→    Vector Database
   266→                          Real-World Example: A super-smart filing cabinet: search by meaning, not filename.
   267→                         Combines with: Embedding, Knowledge Base, RAG
   268→                         Common mistakes: Using a traditional database when vector DB is needed; Not indexing data before storing;
   269→                         Assuming vector DBs work like keyword search
   270→                         Reflection: If your AI can never find the right document, which element might be misconfigured?
   271→
   272→
   273→                       Knowledge Base A curated store of information your AI can search in real time. Unlike training data, it can
   274→        Kb             be updated anytime.
   275→    Knowledge Base
   276→                          Real-World Example: Update a price, and the AI immediately gives the correct answer.
   277→                         Combines with: Vector Database, RAG, Guardrails
   278→                         Common mistakes: Dumping unstructured data without organising; Not keeping the KB up to date; Expecting AI to
   279→                         know things not in the KB
   280→                         Reflection: If AI gives outdated info, do you retrain the model or update the knowledge base?
   281→
   282→
   283→                       RAG Retrieval Augmented Generation. Combines a knowledge base + model so AI answers using YOUR
   284→        Rg             information.
   285→         RAG
   286→                          Real-World Example: Without RAG: generic answers. With RAG: AI searches your handbook, retrieves the
   287→                          section, then generates an answer from your specific policy.
   288→                         Combines with: Knowledge Base, Vector Database, Embedding
   289→                         Common mistakes: Building chatbots without RAG and expecting business knowledge; Confusing RAG with
   290→                         fine-tuning; Not testing retrieval quality before deploying
   291→                         Reflection: Can you explain the difference between an AI that knows general info and one that knows YOUR
   292→                         info?
   293→
   294→
   295→                       Fine-Tuning Training an existing model on your specific data to change how it behaves. More advanced
   296→         Ft            than RAG, but powerful for specialised outputs.
   297→      Fine-Tuning
   298→                          Real-World Example: Need an AI that always writes in your brand's exact voice? Fine-tuning on hundreds of
   299→                          past communications achieves that.
   300→                         Combines with: Training Data, Model, Parameters
   301→                         Common mistakes: Using fine-tuning when RAG would suffice; Fine-tuning on too little data; Not evaluating
   302→                         fine-tuning vs. RAG first
   303→                         Reflection: If you need AI to sound exactly like your brand every time, would you choose RAG or fine-tuning?
   304→
   305→

   341→       Mm              from zero.

   344→                          once, and every future visit it already knows you.

   346→                         Common mistakes: Building chatbots with no memory = impersonal experience; Storing too much and hitting window

   869→ Part 3: Compounds
   870→ Individual elements are powerful, but their true value emerges when they combine into compounds. In chemistry,
   871→ hydrogen and oxygen form water. In AI, Tokens, Prompts, Models, RAG, Vector Databases, and Workflows
   872→ combine to form a Custom AI Assistant. Understanding compounds is how you move from knowing about AI to
   873→ building with AI.
   874→

   879→
   880→
   881→
   882→
   883→ Compound 1: The Custom AI Assistant
   884→ Formula: Token + Prompt + Model + RAG + Vector DB + Knowledge Base + Guardrails + System Prompt
   885→
   886→ This is the most common compound in AI business. A custom AI assistant that knows your company's
   887→ information, speaks in your brand's voice, and stays within safe boundaries. It is the backbone of customer
   888→ support, internal helpdesks, and sales assistants. Without RAG, the AI only knows general information. Without
   889→ guardrails, it might say something damaging. Without a system prompt, it has no consistent personality. Each
   890→ element plays an essential role in making the compound work reliably.
   891→

   894→ Compound 2: The Automated Lead Pipeline
   895→ Formula: Webhook + Workflow + Agent + Function Calling + API + Human-in-the-Loop
   896→
   897→ A lead fills in a form. A webhook triggers a workflow. An AI agent scores the lead using function calling to check
   898→ the CRM via API. If the score is high, it drafts a personalised email. A human reviews (Human-in-the-Loop),
   899→ approves, and the email sends automatically. What used to take 30 minutes of manual work now takes 30 seconds,
   900→

   904→
   905→
   906→ with higher quality and zero missed follow-ups. This compound turns AI from a conversation tool into a revenue
   907→ engine.

   912→ Formula: Multi-Agent + Orchestration + Prompt Engineering + Make/n8n + Knowledge Base + AI Strategy
   913→
   914→ A research agent gathers data from your knowledge base. A writing agent drafts content using prompt engineering
   915→ best practices. An editing agent polishes for brand voice. Orchestration coordinates the sequence. Make or n8n
   916→ handles the automation triggers. Your AI strategy ensures the content aligns with business goals. The result:
   917→ scaled, consistent, high-quality content production that would require an entire team to match manually.
